In the first part, we have: z=1, k=m. By the invariant: z = product of m..k and m <= k <= n. Because we initialize k=m, the invariant begins as z = product of m..m, which is one element (not empty). However, the product of one element is whatever the element is (not necessarily 1). For example, if element m was 5, the product of m..m is 5. In the other case, we say z is the sum of m..k-1. We initialize k=m, so z is originally the sum of m..m-1. The sum of an empty set is 0 so our initialization of k and z satisfies the invariant.
