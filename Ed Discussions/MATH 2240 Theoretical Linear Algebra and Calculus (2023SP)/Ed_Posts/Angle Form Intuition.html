If $\alpha$ is the angle form defined in lecture and $\gamma:[a, b]\to \mathbb R^2$ is a $C^1$ path, how do you see from the formula for the integral of $\alpha$ over $\gamma$ $$\int_\gamma \alpha =\int_a^b \frac{-\gamma_2(t) \gamma_1^{\prime}(t)+\gamma_1(t) \gamma_2^{\prime}(t)}{\gamma_1(t)^2+\gamma_2(t)^2} d t$$ that it represents the increment in the angle between the positive $x$ axis and the vector $\gamma(t)$ from $t = a$ to $t = b$?

Author: Bryan Lu (admin)

Vote Count: 1

One way you can intuitively see it is the following: imagine you could reparameterize $\gamma_1(t)$ and $\gamma_2(t)$ in "polar coordinates" as $\gamma_1(t) = r(t) \cos \theta(t)$ and $\gamma_2(t) = r(t) \sin \theta(t)$, where $r : [a, b] \to [0, \infty)$ and $\theta(t) : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ probably at least be $C^1$ (there's some Inverse Function Theorem argument needed here that can go through if your functions are nice enough). Then with a little bit of computation, you see that the form you get here evaluates to $\theta'(t)$ from some trigonometric manipulation. Then, the interpretation of this integral as an angular displacement follows naturally from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

You can show that the "angle function" $\theta(t)$ is $C^1$ by proving that $\theta(t)=\int_a^t\alpha$, the integral of the angle form along $\gamma$, taken from time $a$ to time $t$. There is a detailed discussion in Section 4.3 of my online lecture notes. (This is not required material for Math 2240.)
