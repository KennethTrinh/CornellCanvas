As far as I've understood from the book, I take that manifolds are not necessarily bounded. Does this mean that if we have a closed non bounded manifold (suppose the plane $x=0$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$) then it will have $3$-dimensional volume $0$, despite it being unbounded?

Author: Luke Serafin (staff)

Yes, a plane in $\mathbb R^3$ will have two-dimensional volume zero. This was incorporated into the definition of $k$-dimensional volume (5.2.1); clause (2) was not included in the original definition of volume in $\mathbb R^n$. Thus our definition of volume has changed now that we have seen Lebesgue integrals (or at least that is my interpretation of the authors' choice of terminology).

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 3

Unfortunately Definition 5.2.1 requires the subset $X$ to be bounded. Otherwise infinitely many dyadic cubes will intersect $X$ and the sum in equation (5.2.1) will be infinite, so $X$ cannot have $k$-dimensional volume $0$. In particular a plane in $\mathbf{R}^3$ does not have $3$-dimensional volume $0$. Proposition 5.2.2 in the book is slightly misstated. It should say: if $X$ is a compact subset of an $m$-manifold $M$ in $\mathbf{R}^n$, then $X$ has $k$-dimensional volume $0$ for any $k>m$. However, there is a more general notion of having $k$-dimensional measure zero. An $m$-dimensional linear subspace of $\R^n$ has $k$-dimensional measure $0$ for any $k>m$.
