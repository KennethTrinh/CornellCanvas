For LTI, you're supposed to see that the I/O mapping is in the standard LTI form of $S ( x ) = h _ 0 * x +$ some shift/identity terms, where $h _ 0$ is a decent signal, in this case $h _ 0 ( t ) = e ^ t u ( t + 1 )$. FYI "0 maps to 0" isn't quite enough to show linearity.

If something's orthogonal to a bunch of $w$'s, it's also orthogonal to any linear combo of those $w$'s. In this case, you can check that the indicated expression is orthogonal to $w _ k$, $1 \leq k \leq 3$, just by plug-in.

Author: David Delchamps (admin)

Vote Count: 0

Take the inner product of that expression with $w _ 1$. You get

$\langle v , w _ 1 \rangle - \sum _ {k = 1} ^ 3 \langle v , w _ k \rangle \langle w _ k , w _ 1 \rangle = \langle v , w _ 1 \rangle - \langle v , w _ 1 \rangle = 0$,

where the second-to last equality holds because the $w _ k$ are orthonormal.

Intuition: the summation is the orthogonal projection of $v$ onto ${\rm span} ( \{ w _ 1 , w _ 2 , w _ 3 \} )$, so $v$ minus the sum is orthogonal to all three $w$'s.
