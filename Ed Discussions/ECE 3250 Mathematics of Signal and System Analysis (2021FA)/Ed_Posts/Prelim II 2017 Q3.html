Just to add: a single signal is never closed under anything. "Closed under [fill in operation]" applies to sets of things on which [operation] is defined. For us, the bulk of our encounters with this have been assertions about whether a set $X$ of signals is

= closed under linear combos, meaning that when $x _ 1$ and $x _ 2$ are in $X$, then so is $c _ 1 x _ 1 + c _ 2 x _ 2$ for all constants $c _ 1$ and $c _ 2$

= closed under shifting, meaning that when $x \in X$ and $t _ o \in \mathbb{R}$ (or $k _ o \in \mathbb{Z}$ in discrete time), then ${\rm Shift} _ {t _ o} ( x ) \in X$ (or ${\rm Shift} _ {k _ o} ( x ) \in X$).

As far as I understand, to be closed under linear combos doesn't mean that the signal itself is linear, just that it can undergo linear combinations. If x1 and x2 are both in the given set,

x1 = t^2 for 0<=t<5 else zero

x2 = 2t^2 for 0<=t<5 else zero

We can make x3 a linear combo of x1 and x2 as follows

x3 = x1 + x2 = 3t^2 0<=t<5 else zero

Here you can see that the resulting signal x3 still belongs to the set of all decent finite-duration continuous-time signals. Another linear combo would be simply multiplying x1 by 2, the solution would still be in the set.
