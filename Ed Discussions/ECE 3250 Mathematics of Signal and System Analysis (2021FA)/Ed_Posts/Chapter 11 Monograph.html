When $f$ is a continuous function of $t$ and $b < t _ 1 < c$ , $\int _ b ^ c \delta ( t - t _ 1 ) f ( t ) d t = f ( t _ 1)$.

Same thing works for functions of $\omega$. I.e. when $f$ is a continuous function of $\omega$ and $b < \omega _ 1 < c$, then $\int _ b ^ c \delta ( \omega - \omega _ 1 ) f ( \omega ) d \omega = f ( \omega _ 1 )$.

This follows from doing the $p _ a$-thing --- let's do it for $t$-functions:

for $a$ small enough, $\frac{1}{a} \int _ b ^ c p _ a ( t - t _ 1 ) f ( t ) d t = \frac{1}{a} \int _ {t _ 1 - a / 2} ^ {t _ 1 + a / 2} f ( t ) d t$, which equals the average value of $f ( t )$ over the interval $t _ 1 - a / 2 \leq t \leq t _ 1 + a / 2$, which converges to $f ( t _ 1 )$ as $a \rightarrow 0$.

In the case you cite, $b = - \pi$, $c = \pi$, $f ( \omega ) = e ^ {j n \omega}$, and $\omega _ 1 = \omega _ o - 2 \pi k ^ *$. The monograph has a little mistake that doesn't change the answer, i.e. it has $+ 2 \pi k ^ *$ instead of $- 2 \pi k ^ *$.
