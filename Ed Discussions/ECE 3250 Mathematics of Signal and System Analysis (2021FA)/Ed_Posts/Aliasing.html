Author: David Delchamps (admin)

Vote Count: 1

Turns out that's prohibited. Only integer powers of complex numbers make unambiguous sense. Obviously this $x _ c$ is not identically equal to 1, so your proposal can't be right --- -what goes wrong? If $z _ o = R e ^ {j \theta}$, it's tempting to say $z _ o ^ {1 / 2} = \sqrt{R} e ^ {j \theta / 2}$, but that's only one of two possible answers. Things get even weirder when the powers aren't rational, but here's and example the kind of error that arises when you don't obey the integer-powers-only rule. We know that $e ^ {j 4 \pi ^ 2} \neq 1$. But how come, since $\left ( e ^ {j 2 \pi} \right ) ^ { 2 \pi} = 1 ^ {2 \pi} = 1$? Complex logs give rise to the difficulties. Like, with real numbers, we can say, $x ^ t = e ^ {t \ln x}$ when $x > 0$. But when $z _ o$ is complex, it has many logs, like the $z _ o$ above has the following ones, among others: $\ln R + j \theta$, $\ln R + j ( \theta + 2 \pi )$, $\ln R + j ( \theta + 4 \pi )$, etc. And if you wanted to calculate $z _ o ^ t$ by using logs, you'd get a different answer for each $e ^ {t ( {\rm one \; of \; those} )}$.
