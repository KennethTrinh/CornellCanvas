As for non-periodicity of the sequence you ask about, it turns out to be periodic only for rare values of $\omega _ o$. Like if you want $e ^ {j n \omega _ o} = e ^ {j ( n + K ) \omega _ o}$ for some integer $K$, you need $e ^ {j K \omega _ o} = 1$, so $K \omega _ o$ has to be a multiple of $2 \pi$, which happens only for a countable set of $\omega _ o$-values.

Regarding the last paragraph, it's not true in continuous time that if $x$ has frequency content at $\Omega _ o$ it also has frequency content at $\Omega _ o + 2 \pi m$ for nonzero integers $m$. Like, $x ( t ) = e ^ {j \Omega _ o t}$ has frequency content only at $\Omega _ o$ and at no other frequency. Also, nothing "good" or "bad" about any of this stuff. :-)

Author: David Delchamps (admin)

$x ( t ) = e ^ {j \Omega _ o t}$ is indeed a periodic function of $t$. For any $\Omega _ o$-value you can find infinitely many $T$-values for which $x ( t - T ) = x ( t )$ for all $t$. Any such $T$ is an integer multiple of $2 \pi / \Omega _ o$.

In discrete time, $K$ has to be an integer, and $\omega _ o K$ needs to be a multiple of $2 \pi$ --- only for certain $\omega _ o$-values can you find such a $K$. It's the integer constraint on $K$ that distinguishes the discrete-time case.

Your last equation is incorrect because it requires that $e ^ {j 2 \pi m t} = 1$ for all $t$, which holds only when $m = 0$.
