Author: David Delchamps (admin)

(vi) $x _ c$ needs to be bandlimited within $\pi / T$, and it's not necessarily if it's only bandlimited to within $2 \pi / T$.

(viii) One of the mantras: "if the samples could have come from a pure sinusoid, the sinc-function $T$-interpolation of them will be a pure sinusoid no matter what $T$ is." We demonstrated that in class using pictures of signals going through boxes.

(ix) Various approaches work, including scaling laws for Fourier transforms, which is the one I can think of now. One way to think of it without scaling laws: the discrete-time signal $x$ is now $y _ c$ sampled every $T / 3$, or at angular frequency $6 \pi / T$, which is faster than the Nyquist rate of $y _ c$. Thus no aliasing occurs during sampling, so we'll get $x _ R = x _ c$.

Author: David Delchamps (admin)

Vote Count: 1

(iii) is false because $\widehat{X}$ is $2 \pi$-periodic, not $2 \pi / T$-periodic.

(iii) is true because if $x _ c$ is bandlimited to within $\pi / 2 T$, then on the $\omega$-interval $[ - \pi , \pi ]$ $\widehat{X} ( \omega )$ is nonzero only out to $\omega = \pm \pi // 2$. This results from the $\omega \leftrightarrow \Omega T$ scaling.
