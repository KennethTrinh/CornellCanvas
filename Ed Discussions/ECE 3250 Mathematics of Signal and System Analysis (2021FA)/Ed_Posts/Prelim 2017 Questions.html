For 6(iv), if $V$ has dimension exactly 5, then the set of $w$'s is a basis for $V$, and nothing in $V$ is orthogonal to all of them.

For 5(ii), $\mathcal{W}$ isn't necessarily a complete orthonormal set in $V$, which it needs to be for (ii) to hold for all $v \in V$.

Author: David Delchamps (admin)

Vote Count: 1

An orthonormal basis for a finite-dimensional inner product space is the simplest example of a complete orthonormal set. If $V$ in this problem has dimension 5, then for every $v \in V$ we have

$v = \sum _ {k = 1} ^ 5 \langle v , w _ k \rangle w _ k$.

It follows that $v = 0$ if $\langle v , w _ k \rangle = 0$ for all $k$.

Author: David Delchamps (admin)

To the first question, Yes.

To the second, "orthonormal basis" is kind of a loose term to apply to an infinite-dimensional vector space, but essentially it's like this: a complete orthonormal set in a Hilbert space (not just any inner product space) is like an orthonormal basis for the space in the sense that you can write every $v$ in the space as an "infinite linear combo" of the basis vectors.
