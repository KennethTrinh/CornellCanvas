Hi, if you want to know the exact carrier concentration, you can start from the charge neutrality equation. I believe Professor El-Ghazaly will cover this soon. All the charge in your semiconductor adds to zero. In the Equation 1, n0 is the electron concentration in equilibrium, p0 is the hole concentration in equilibrium, Na is the acceptor (dopant with valence electron less than 4) concentration, Nd is the donor (dopant with valence electron more than 4) concentration. I will make two assumptions here. First, the donor is Group 5 and the acceptor is Group 3; one atom provides one electron or hole. Second, the dopants are fully ionized; the extra carrier concentration introduced by the dopants is equal to the dopant concentration. Each donor atom provides an electron to silicon, and then it gets positively charged. Similarly, each acceptor atom becomes negatively charged when it provides a hole (equivalent to getting one more electron). So to achieve charge neutrality, the sum of all 4 charge should go to zero (Equation 1). According to the mass action law that was covered today, ni^2 = n0*p0. Equation 4 is the electron concentration at equilibrium. If there are both donors and acceptors, the compensation doping effect (covered today) can be seen; Nd-Na. You can do the same thing for holes. According to the equation 4, if the dopant density is much larger than ni, you can ignore ni. If not, you can calculate it from the equation.
