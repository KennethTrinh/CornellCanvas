In the ideal model, a diode acts as a short circuit when forward biased. In this case, Vout does not depend on Vin as you see from the circuit diagram. When Vin is large, D1 is turned off, so there's no voltage drop across R, and thus the voltage at the cathode of D1 is Vin. Then you know the condition to make it forward biased.

Author: Hyunjea Lee (staff)

Vote Count: 0

For the first part, Vout is in parallel with 2V voltage source.

When I said large, that means it's some value like 3 or 4 V. A diode acts as an open circuit when it's reverse biased. If D1 is reverse biased, there's no loop to flow current. There's no current across R, and there's no voltage drop across R.

You may start from two cases: D1 forward biased and D1 reverse biased. Then you try to find the threshold condition. Let's assume Vin = 4 V is applied and D1 is turned off in the beginning. As you increase Vin, the voltage across D1 changes: 2V - Vin. Then you know at which Vin D1 becomes turned on. You get the expression for Vout at the two extreme cases (Vin = 4 or -4 V), find the threshold, and plot the two equations separated at the threshold voltage.
