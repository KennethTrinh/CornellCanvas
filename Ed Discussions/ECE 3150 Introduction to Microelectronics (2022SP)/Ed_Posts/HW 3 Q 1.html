Author: Max McCarthy (staff)

Vote Count: 1

The impedance looking into the emitter of a BJT can be approximated as $\frac{1}{g_m}$. If you draw a small signal model, you have two impedances at the Emitter node (3 if you consider $r_0$ from the Early Effect). One is $r_{\pi}$ going to the Base, and the other goes to the Collector, $\frac{1}{g_m}$. You can also further treat the Base and Collector nodes as "AC ground". This means you can treat DC voltages as ground in AC analysis. Since $ r_{\pi} = \frac{\beta}{g_m}$, and we assume that $\beta$ is large, the parallel combination formed be these two impedances simplifies to just $\frac{1}{g_m}$ (because $\frac{1}{g_m} << \frac{\beta}{g_m}$) Note that this corresponds to just one of the two BJTs; consider how you might combine the impedances of both devices to derive an expression for the total input impedance.
