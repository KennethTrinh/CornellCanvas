(1) and (2) are indeed the pesky boundary case. I'm not sure how you mean "what should be done in this case." I'll give you a handout toward the end of the course that addresses it. It turns out that if you sample $A \cos ( 2 \pi f _ o t + \phi )$ at frequency $f _ s = 2 f _ o$ and put the resulting discrete-time signal through an ideal D-to-C with interpolation interval $T _ s = 1 / f _ s$, what comes out is the signal $( A / 2 ) \cos ( 2 \pi f _ o t + \phi ) + ( A / 2 ) \cos ( 2 \pi f _ o t - \phi )$. In the case of the movie, $f _ o = 12$ and $\phi = \pi / 2$, and the output of the D-to-C is half clockwise rotation plus half counterclockwise rotation. To prove this properly requires more math than ECE 2200 covers. As for your questions (3) and (4). the only way I know how to do it reliably is go full Euler on everything. And remember, the product of two cosines need not even be periodic if the frequencies aren't harmonically related.
