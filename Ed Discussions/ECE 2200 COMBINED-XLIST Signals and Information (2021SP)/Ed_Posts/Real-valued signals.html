I have a general question based on the lecture content up until now. When we say a signal is a "real-valued" signal, does this imply that it is continuous-valued?

I know "real-valued" means x(t) could take on a value in the space of real numbers, but could it be restricted to certain real numbers and be discrete-valued?

It could be restricted to certain real values. For example a square wave that for all integers $n$ satisfies

$x ( t ) = \left \{ \begin{array}{cl} 1 & {\rm whem} \; n \leq t < n + 1 / 2 \\ 0 & {\rm when} \; n + 1 / 2 \leq t < n + 1 \end{array} \right .$

is a real-valued signal that takes on only 0 and 1 as values. Usually we say "real-valued" when we want to contrast with "complex-valued."
