You've hit on what I call the Reverse Sampling Theorem. It's essentially dual to the actual Sampling Theorem. Here's the parallel.

Sampling Theorem: If you know that a continuous-time signal is bandlimited to within $f _ {\rm max}$, then you can reconstruct the signal completely from its samples $x ( n T _ s )$ provided $f _ s = 1 / T _ s > 2 f _ {\rm. max}$.

Reverse Sampling Theorem: If you know that $\widehat{X} ( \widehat{\omega} )$ is the DTFT of a discrete-time signal that has duration at most $N$, then you can reconstruct $\widehat{X} ( \widehat{\omega} )$ completely from samples $\widehat{X} ( k 2 \pi / M )$, $0 \leq k < M$, provided $M \geq N$.

In the Sampling Theorem, your "prior knowledge" is that the signal is bandlimited. In the Reverse, your prior knowledge is that that function $\widehat{X} ( \widehat{\omega} )$ is the DTFT of a finite-duration signal. In both theorems, you're amazed that you can rebuild a continuous thing out of a discrete sampling of it.

The Reverse theorem requires only finitely many samples as opposed to a countable infinity in the Sampling Theorem because $\widehat{X} ( \widehat{\omega} )$ is $2 \pi$-periodic.
