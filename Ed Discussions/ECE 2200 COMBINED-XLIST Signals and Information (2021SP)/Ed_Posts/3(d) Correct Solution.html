After scratching my head about this problem for a while I feel like the correct solution also has errors with it: Copy-pasting for reference Solution to a version of the problem that works as planned: Change the 9 to 3 — i.e. set Ts = 1/(7π − 3), so you form x[n] by sampling x(t) at frequency fs = 7π − 3 Hz. When you pass x[n] through the D-to-C, what emerges is the sum of three terms. Each term will be the principal continuous-time alias of the corresponding term in x(t) w.r.t. the sampling frequency fs. To compute those, proceed as follows: – The −5 is its own principal continuous-time alias w.r.t. fs. This one is fine – The 4 cos(2π(t)t) is also its own principal continuous-time alias w.r.t. fs because fs = 7π − 3 > 2 × 4 = 8. Alternatively, since you sampled the 4 cos(2π(6)t) faster than its Nyquist rate, when you put those samples through the D-to-C, the original signal will come out. 4 cos(2π(t)t) should be 4 cos(2π(6)t - π/2), thus the our fs calculation should be 7π − 3 > 2 × 6 = 12 – The term of frequency 7π is not its own principal continuous-time alias w.r.t. fs — fs < 7π, so we’ve sampled this term nowhere near its Nyquist rate. To find the principal continuous-time alias of this term w.r.t. fs, step its frequency down by integer multiples of fs until you land in the interval (−fs/2, fs/2). It takes only one step —you land at 7π − fs = 3. Thus the principal continuous-time alias of this term w.r.t. fs is 2√ 2 cos(2π(3)r+π/4). This seems fine as well The signal that exits the D-to-C is therefore q(t) = −5 + 4 cos(2π(6)t) + 2√ 2 cos(2π(3)t + π/4) . This signal is periodic because its terms’ frequencies are harmonically related, and its fundamental frequency is the greatest common integer divisor of 3 and 6, namely 6. The greatest common integer divisor of 3 and 6 is 3 not 6, so f = 3. I could be completely wrong about the above but some issues I noticed with the correct solution.
