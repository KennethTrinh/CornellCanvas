Great answer Nicola! Just a note that all of your arguments/definitions are for a heuristic being admissible not both admissible and consistent. However, a heuristic only needs to be admissible to find an optimal solution! A heuristic is admissible if it never over estimates the cost to the goal. Symbolically, for all states $n$, $h(n) \leq h^*(n)$, where $h(n)$ is the heuristic and $h*(n)$ is the true distance to the goal state. A heuristic is consistent/monotone if it obeys the triangle inequality, i.e. the heuristic value at a node is never less than the cost of travelling to another node plus the heuristic value at that node. Symbolically, for all $n, n'$, $h(n) \leq c(n,n') + h(n')$, where $c(n,n')$ is the cost to move from state $n$ to state $n'$.
