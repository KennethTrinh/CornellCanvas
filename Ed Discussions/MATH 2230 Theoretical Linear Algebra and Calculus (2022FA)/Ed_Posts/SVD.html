For SVD, if the diagonal entries of $\Sigma$ become 0 for indices $(i,i)$ where $i>r$, does it matter what goes in the columns past $r$ in $P$ and $Q$ for $A$ to equal $P \Sigma Q^T$?

----------- REPLIES -----------

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 1

It doesn't matter, as long as the matrices $P$ and $Q$ are orthogonal (i.e. their columns are orthonormal).

Suppose you have determined the first $r$ columns $\mathbf{v}_1,\mathbf{v}_2,\dotsc,\mathbf{v}_r$ of $Q$. Then the first $r$ columns $\mathbf{w}_1,\mathbf{w}_2,\dotsc,\mathbf{w}_r$ of $P$ are determined by $\mathbf{w}_i=\frac1{\sigma_i}A\mathbf{v}_i$.

For the columns $\mathbf{v}_{r+1},\dotsc,\mathbf{v}_n$ of $Q$ you can take any orthonormal basis of $\operatorname{ker}(A)=\operatorname{im}(A^T)^\perp$.

For the columns $\mathbf{w}_{r+1},\dotsc,\mathbf{w}_m$ of $P$ you can take any orthonormal basis of $\operatorname{ker}(A^T)=\operatorname{im}(A)^\perp$.

If all the columns past $r$ in $\Sigma$ are $\textbf{0}$, wouldn't $P \Sigma$ have all of its columns past $r$ be $\textbf{0}$ no matter what?

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 0

Sure, but we want $P$ to be orthogonal, which forces the last $m-r$ columns of $P$ to be an o.n. basis of the kernel of $A^T$.

One reason why we want $P$ to be orthogonal is that we want $A^T=Q\Sigma^TP^T$ to be a SVD of $A^T$.
