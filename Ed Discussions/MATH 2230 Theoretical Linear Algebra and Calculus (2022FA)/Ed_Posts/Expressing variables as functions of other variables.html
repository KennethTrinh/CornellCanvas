If I row reduce an $A$ and find its pivot variables and free variables, then I can express the pivot variables as a function of the free variables in the sense that, for any choice of values for the free variables, there are unique values for the pivot variables such that the corresponding vector is in the null space of $A$.

Is it possible for there also to be different set of variables which can be expressed as a function of the others in this sense? And if so, is there a good way of finding them?

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 2

Which variables are pivot variables and which are free depends on the order of the variables. Renumber the variables, i.e. permute the columns of your matrix, and you will wind up with different sets of free and pivot variables.

For "most" matrices of rank $r$, any $r$ out of the $n$ variables can serve as pivot variables. In other words, for "most" matrices of rank $r$ any $r$ of its columns will be independent. The reason is that for an $r$-tuple of columns of $A$ to be dependent, $A$ must satisfy a number of algebraic equations, namely the determinants of certain $r\times r$-submatrices of $A$ must be $0$.

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 1

No, that's not quite correct. The hypothesis of the ImpFT can fail, but the conclusion may still be valid. One can easily produce $F$'s such that $DF(\mathbf{a})$ is the zero matrix for every $\mathbf{a}$ in the solution set $M=F^{-1}(\mathbf{0})$, and yet $M$ is a nice manifold.

Here's how to make such a function: start with an

$$F=\begin{pmatrix}F_1\\\vdots\\F_m\end{pmatrix}$$

such that $DF(\mathbf{a})$ has rank $m$ for every $\mathbf{a}\in M$ and replace it with

$$G=\begin{pmatrix}F_1^2\\\vdots\\F^2_m\end{pmatrix}.$$

Then $M=G^{-1}(\mathbf{0})$ but $DG(\mathbf{a})=0$ for all $\mathbf{a}\in M$.

Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 1

Not for general differentiable functions $F$. For special types of equations there exist methods to get around the limitations of the implicit function theorem. For example, if $F(x,y)$ is a polynomial in $x$ and $y$ there is a method found by Newton (now known as Puiseux expansion) to express $y$ as a power series in fractional powers of $x$, or $x$ as a power series in fractional powers of $y$. As a simple example, $x^3-y^2=0$ has solution $y=x^{3/2}$ or $x=y^{2/3}$.
