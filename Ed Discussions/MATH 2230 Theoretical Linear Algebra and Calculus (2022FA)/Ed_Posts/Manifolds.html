If $M\subseteq \mathbb R^n$ and each $\vec a \in M$ has an open neighborhood $V$ such that $V\cap M$ is the image of a $C^1$, one-to-one function $\gamma :U\subseteq \mathbb R^k \to \mathbb R^n$, and $U$ is open and $D\gamma (\vec u)$ is one-to-one for all $u\in U$, does it follow that $M$ is a $k$ dimensional manifold?

If so, how can I show this? I tried defining a function $F:V\to \mathbb R^{n-k}$ by $F(\gamma(\vec u)) = \vec 0$ for $\vec u \in U$, so that $M\cap V = F^{-1}(\vec 0)$, but I don't know if $DF(\gamma(\vec u))$ will be onto, and I don't know how to define $F(\vec x)$ for $\vec x \in V\backslash M$ in such a way that $F$ is $C^1$.

No, it does not.  Here is a counterexample.  Let $k=1$, $U=(0,3\pi/2)$,

$$\gamma(u)=\begin{pmatrix}\cos u\\\cos u\sin u\end{pmatrix}.$$

You can verify that this map satisfies all your conditions.  Is the image $\gamma(U)$ a manifold?  You can plot the curve using software such as wolframalpha.

But the situation can be fixed by adding the following requirement to your maps $\gamma$.   Viewed as a map from $U$ onto $\gamma(U)$, $\gamma$ is bijective, and therefore has an inverse $\gamma^{-1}\colon\gamma(U)\to U$.  We require $\gamma^{-1}$ to be continuous.  Under this extra condition the answer to your question is “yes”.
