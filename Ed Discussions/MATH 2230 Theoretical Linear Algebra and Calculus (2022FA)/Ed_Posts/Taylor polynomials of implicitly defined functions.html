Suppose $F(x, y) = 0$ defines $x$ as a function $g(y)$ of $y$ near $y = a$. Then (i) for small $h$, $F(g(a+h),a+h) = 0$ and (ii) by taylor's theorem, $|T^k_{g,a}(a+h) - g(a+h)| = o(|h^k|)$. In class, we said that

$$F(T^k_{g,a}(a+h), a+h) = o(|h^k|)$$

Does this follow from (i) and (ii)? And if so, how?

Better write $o(|\mathbf{h}|^k)$, because $\mathbf{h}$ is a vector.

The answer to your question is "yes", and here is the reason. I'll replace your $k$ with an $m$ because I will use $\mathbf{k}$ for something else. Let $T_F$ be the $m$th order Taylor polynomial for $F$ at $(\mathbf{b},\mathbf{a})$. Then

$$F(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{k},\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})=T_F(\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{k},\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})+o\biggl(\biggl|\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{k}\\\mathbf{h}\end{pmatrix}\biggr|^m\biggr).$$

Let $T_g$ be the $m$th order Taylor polynomial for the implicitly defined function $g$ at $\mathbf{a}$. We know that $F(g(\mathbf{y}),\mathbf{y})=\mathbf{0}$ for all $\mathbf{y}$, so from the chain rule for Taylor polynomials it follows that, up to terms of order $>m$, $T_F(T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}),\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})$ is the Taylor polynomial of the constant function $\mathbf{0}$, so

$$T_F(T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}),\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})=o(|\mathbf{h}|^m).$$ Now we plug $\mathbf{b}+\mathbf{k}=T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})$ into the first equation, i.e. $\mathbf{k}=T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})-\mathbf{b}=O(|\mathbf{h}|)$. (Here we use big O notation: $f=O(g)$ means $|f(x)|\le Mg(x)$ for some $M\ge0$.) We get

$$F(T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}),\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})=T_F(T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}),\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})+o(|\mathbf{h}|^m),$$

because

$$o\biggl(\biggl|\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{k}\\\mathbf{h}\end{pmatrix}\biggr|^m\biggr)=o\bigl(|\mathbf{k}|^m+|\mathbf{h}|^m\bigr)=o\bigl(O(|\mathbf{h}|)^m+|\mathbf{h}|^m\bigr)=o(|\mathbf{h}|^m).$$

Combining this with the second equation we get

$$F(T_g(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}),\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h})=o(|\mathbf{h}|^m)+o(|\mathbf{h}|^m)=o(|\mathbf{h}|^m).$$
