For part (a), $CX$ is the union of all lines through the origin and a point $(x, y, 1)$ such that $(x, y) \in X$, so it seems like it follows that $CX = \{(x, y, z) \mid z\neq 0 \text{ and } (x/z, y/z)\in X\}\cup\{(0,0,0)\}$ (I put my reasoning at the end). But intuitively it seems like this set should be closed, so what is added by taking its closure? Is the question just asking me to include $(0, 0, 0)$?

Reasoning:

if $(x, y, z)\in CX$, then $(x, y, z) $ is on some line through the origin and a point $(x_0, y_0, 1)$ such that $(x_0, y_0) \in X$, so it is a scalar multiple $t(x_0, y_0, 1)$ of that point. If $(x, y, z)= (0, 0, 0)$, then there's nothing to show. Otherwise, $x = tx_0$, $y = ty_0$, and $z = tz_0$, so since at least one of $x,y,z\neq 0$ and each of them is a multiple of $t$, it follows that $t\neq 0$. Thus, we can divide $x = tx_0$ and $y = ty_0$ by $ z=t$ to get $(x/z, y/z) = (x_0, y_0)\in X$

Conversely, the point $(0, 0, 0)$ is clearly in $CX$ and if $z \neq 0$ and $(x/z,y/z)\in X$, then $(x, y, z) = z(x/z, y/z, 1)$ is on the line through the origin and $(x/z, y/z, 1)$, so it's in $CX$.

No, the cone $CX$ is in general not a closed set.  See part (b) for an example where $CX\ne\overline{CX}$.

Your description of $CX$ is not wrong, but in part (a) you are asked to give an equation.  Your description is not an equation.

You are giving away too many details. This discussion forum is not intended for posting nearly complete solutions.

That said, let me answer your question by saying

  • The origin is contained in $CX$. But there may be points in the $xy$-plane other than the origin which are contained in the closure of $CX$. See part (b).

  • The equation you wrote is valid away from the $xy$-plane. For a general function $f$ you cannot do better than the equation you wrote. But if $f$ is a polynomial in $x$ and $y$ you can clear denominators and obtain an equation for $\overline{CX}$ that holds everywhere in $\mathbf{R}^3$.
