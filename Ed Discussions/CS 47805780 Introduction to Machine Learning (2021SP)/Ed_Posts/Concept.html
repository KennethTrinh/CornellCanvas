So

v_{ }=\sigma\left(s_{ }\right)

We know that v is some n-dimensional vector. Remember the definition of a gradient of a vector (x) with respect to another vector (y) is as follows (first expression). the second expression is by plugging in values for x and y (with v and s). The instructors explain it in the previous video to the slides you posted

\begin{bmatrix}\frac{dx_1}{dy_1}&...&\frac{dx_1}{dy_m}\\\vdots&\vdots&\vdots\\\frac{dv_n}{dy_1}&...&\frac{dx_n}{dy_m}\end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}\frac{d\left(\sigma\left(s_1\right)\right)}{ds_1}&...&\frac{d\left(\sigma\left(s_1\right)\right)}{ds_n}\\\vdots&...&\vdots\\\frac{d\left(\sigma\left(s_1\right)\right)}{ds_1}&...&\frac{d\left(\sigma\left(s_n\right)\right)}{ds_n}\end{bmatrix} =\begin{bmatrix}\sigma'(s_1)&...&0\\0&...&0\\0&...&\sigma'(s_n)\end{bmatrix} = diag(\sigma'(s))
