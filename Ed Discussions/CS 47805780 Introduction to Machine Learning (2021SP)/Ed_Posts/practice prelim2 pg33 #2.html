You're on the right track, although d means the number of boolean variables, not the length of the OR expression. It's any OR expression of any length, so: 1 variable: x1 would be an option, or x2, or xd -- as well as their negations, like ~x1, ~x2, ~xd. These are all OR functions. 2 variables: x1 or x2, ~x1 or x3, x2 or xd, all count. You can use at most 2 variables. 3 variables: x1 or x3 or x4, etc. ... d variables: x1 or x2 or x3 or ... or xd. But don't forget that all the variables could be negated too.
