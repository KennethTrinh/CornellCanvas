I noticed that, for McNemar's Test, we only need to check whether or not P(W <= w | p=0.5,n=w+1)<0.025 OR P(W <= w | p=0.5,n=w+1)<0.025 but we don't have to check both. However, I don't understand why this is the case. If we are doing a two-sided test (i.e. we only care about whether or not the two error rates are different , but we're not addressing which one is actually greater than the other), don't we need to check the probability at both tails and make sure that the total probability is <0.05? How do we know that it is sufficient to only check one tail?

It's not that the total probability has to be < 0.05. You're checking that your sample value is not extreme on either tail of the distribution. Alternatively, you can think about it as you're checking if your sample value falls outside of the middle 95% of the distribution (i.e. in the bottom 2.5% or the top 2.5%).

Author: Bonnie Akhavan (staff)

For this class, it's sufficient to only test one side as long as you choose the more extreme side to test. It's implied that if the more extreme side fails to reject the hypothesis that their errors are similar, then the less extreme side will do so as well.
