Overview/Hypotheses

Joyce and I are looking at r/AmItheAsshole to characterize a buzzy user (a user that comments in several conversation threads). We hypothesized that: controversial topics will have more buzzy users, buzzy users tend to disagree within the thread, and buzzy users will agree with the final judgement.

Dataset

r/aita 2018: 17,598 posts with 448,946 comments

r/aita 2019: 244,595 posts with 9,902,311 comments

    The initial feasibility test looked at the number of words in a comment and number of comments in a thread in the 2018 dataset. Glancing at some of the posts in the 2018 dataset, it seemed as if most redditors were briefly stating their opinion and that there wasn't the back and forth discussion we were looking for. However, given the growth of the subreddit in 2019, we're hopeful that there are more buzzy users.

    In some of my discussions with Joyce, we talked about the relationship between controversy, topics, tone, and number of comments. What's the relationship between the tone of a post and the number of comments it receives, if there is a relationship. Are certain topics discussed more? Are these controversial?

    For each post in the 2018 and the 2019 dataset, I created a tone analysis figure that describes the overall concreteness, valence, arousal, and dominance for each post. I combined the wordlists referenced by Tao et. al in the "Winning Arguments" to create a dictionary of 13,384 words with concreteness, valence, arousal, and dominance. (This excluded words that were in the concreteness wordlist but not in the valence, arousal, and dominance wordlist and vice versa). For each post I counted the number of word occurrences and normalized for length.

    I was surprised that there were posts within the dataset that didn't have any words in the tone dictionary. One item on my to do list is to go back and see which posts have a [0,0,0,0] vector.

    On disagreement vs agreement:

  • The intuition behind "buzzy users disagree within the thread" was that buzzy users may be buzzy because they are engaging in multiple conversation branches, and that the motivation for doing this is to disagree with someone else's opinions / add their own thoughts. Adding comments that present different or at least varying viewpoints seems like it'd be more interesting than adding comments that are just "yes, I agree 100%".

  • However, for "buzzy users agree more with final judgement", on the whole their passing of ideas across the entire thread means that they may have a larger impact on the overall discussion... or have observed enough of it to see the big points others are raising, which can contribute to their own judgements.

  • These came from casual reading of the sub in its modern state: especially in really controversial posts, there's frequently users who will share ideas that have been discussed in a separate comment thread like "look here, you've missed out on something!!" In general, these two hypotheses fit together with the "buzzy users are more active in controversial overall posts": if a post is controversial, there are more users in favor of each point. Buzzy users thus have more space to disagree with particular comments while spreading ideas throughout the entire conversation tree.
