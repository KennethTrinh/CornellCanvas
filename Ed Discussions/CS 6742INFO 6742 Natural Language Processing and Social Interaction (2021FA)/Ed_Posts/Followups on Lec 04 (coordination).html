The rhetorical reason I brought up the topic-shifting visualization was as an example of a study of topic control as related to power (in this case, interpreted as "dominance", since technically a debate moderator should have more institutional power within the debate than a debate participant.). A paper to look at is: Nguyen, Viet-An, Jordan Boyd-Graber, Philip Resnik, Deborah A. Cai, Jennifer E. Midberry, and Yuanxin Wang. 2014. “Modeling Topic Control to Detect Influence in Conversations Using Nonparametric Topic Models.” Machine Learning 95 (3): 381–421. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10994-013-5417-9. An excerpt from the intro reads: "Among many studied behaviors, topic control and management is considered one of the most effective ways to control the conversation (Planalp and Tracy 1980). Palmer ( 1989) shows that the less related a participants’ utterances are to the immediate topic, the more dominant they are, and then argues, “ the ability to change topical focus, especially given strong cultural and social pressure to be relevant, means having enough interpersonal power to take charge of the agenda. ” Recent work by Rienks et al. ( 2006) also shows that topic change, among other structural patterns discussed above, is the most robust feature in detecting influencers in small group meetings." A visualization of the topic-control example I had in mind is slides 13-14 of Philip Resnik's 2014 talk " I Want to Talk About, Again, My Record On Energy...” Modeling Agendas and Framing in Political Debates and Other Conversations", covering the paper mentioned above (among other papers.) In the slide below, the l_c is showing the model (retrospectively? ) thinking a topic shift has occurred; on the left-hand side bar the columns are (automatically induced)topics, so around line 48 the model has decided that instead of the far-left topic, a different topic is being referred to. The circle around the box that says "energy oil nuclear clean climate cool alternative power" is indicating the keywords most associated with a particular "topic" induced by the model. This next slide shows the tendency for each speaker to topic-shift, according to the model. The grey bars indicate debate moderators/questioners, who one expects to change the topic of the conversation by asking questions on new topics; but Palin is shown to be changing topic as often as one of the moderators (Ifill).
