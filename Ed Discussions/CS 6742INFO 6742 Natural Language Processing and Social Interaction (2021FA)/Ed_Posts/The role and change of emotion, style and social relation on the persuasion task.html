Title: The role and change of emotion, style and social relation on the persuasion task

Author: Fengyang Lin (student)

Date: 2021-09-09T08:32:59.94208+10:00

Category: A1

Vote Count: 0

As indicated in Hovy et al.'s (2021) paper, speaker's attribute such as tone and style can vary according to different situations and tasks, while the relation between speaker and receiver can introduce variation into the resulting text. Do people's emotions and tones change over the persuasion? Do people with different communicative styles and tones differ in their attitude to change their minds? If the OP and the commenter have interacted before, is the OP more likely to be persuaded?

As an extension to the work on language indicators of persuasive arguments by Tan et al. (2016), the project aims to understand the role and change of emotion, style, relation of OPs and commenters during the persuasion task. It should start with using a similar setup in the paper and include the argument-only features. Then sentiment analysis and emotion detection should be conducted for different quarters in the path, and see if the emotion and style will change over the conversation.

The second part of the project is more speaking-style-wise or cluster-wise. A naive way to do so is to divide OPs and commenters into binary groups with arousal and valence based on their overall speaking style and compare the ratio of success in persuasion. It is said that calmer languages are more likely to succeed in persuasion, then what kind of OPs are more easily to be persuaded? Particularly, I noticed that in some of the conversions, people used emoticons like ":)" to express their feelings and attitude. I am interested in learning if people with a more casual speaking style (using emoji, emoticons, text abbreviation, "-" and "()" to add explanation) would behave differently in the persuasion task. Moreover, as emoticons/emojis represent some kind of emotion, does a reply from OP with ":)" serve as a signal for success in persuasion? Given the pilot study, the number of posts and comments with emoticons/emojis is relatively small, but I think it worths taking a look into this attribute. Lastly, I am interested in how the relation (if any) between OPs and commenters will affect the task. If possible, a test will be performed on whether the CP and commenter have interacted before would affect the success of persuasion. Though, I doubt if there will be enough samples for this particular test. Still, I list the hypothesis here and will definitely try to see if it is feasible.

Any comments and suggestions are welcome! If you are also interested in these topics, I am very happy to cooperate. Thanks ^^

    • Problems that I encountered and next steps:

  • The way that I cleaned the data seems to be too naive. After preprocessing, there are still HTML character entities or tags remaining and sometimes it makes it hard to detect the true Emoticons. Examples are as below:

  • For the string [... (**Some text in bold **) ... ] or [... (*** Some text with Hyperlink ***)] , the current process will treat *) as an Emoticon, and it happens several times. I was trying to exclude such kinds of cases, but it seems like I could not always cover everything.

  • Issues with “d: / d; / D: / D;” . Consider two sentences. “Something I liked:…” and “I love it:D” . Sometimes Emoticons hide in the sentence - using whitespace tokenization will lead to missing them. But if not using whitespace tokenization, it will be hard to tell whether it is a true Emoticon or it is just a combination of [a word ends with "d"] + [“;” or “:”] .

  • Tentative solution : Perform text normalization and tokenization based on Chris’s tutorial, which I found is super inspiring and useful! Maybe also manually exclude some cases or adding extra preprocessing steps.

  • The set of OP posts with Emojis is too small - in fact, I could only found two. I was wondering if I am using the wrong dictionary for checking Emoji, or if it is just the case. Similar situation for Emoticons. For the 3456 training dataset, only 192 OP posts contain Emoticons, including some false positive cases. I haven’t done with comments, wish there will be more.

  • Tentative solution : For Emojis, I need to check if there is something that went wrong with my process. Also, I could Include more data - I am only using the data for the paired task and should be able to include more from the whole dataset.
