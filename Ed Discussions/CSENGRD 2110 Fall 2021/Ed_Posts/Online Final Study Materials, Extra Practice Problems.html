While practice problems are helpful to make the exam environment feel more familiar, to really internalize the material you need hands-on experience. So our recommendation would be to practice writing programs at this point.

Pick a past exam problem that you had some trouble with, and write a program to illustrate the concepts being tested. Then tweak the program in various ways and see what changes. Thoroughly analyze it so that you can predict what will happen when you make particular tweaks. Then pick another, unrelated problem, and repeat.

As you identify patterns, think of which definitions or rules in JHT govern them, and see if you can state those rules in your own words. This form of analytical and reflective thinking should solidify the material much better than answering disconnected test problems.

Author: Curran Muhlberger (admin)

Follow-up tip: find ways to visualize what your programs are doing as they run. Adding lots of println statements is an easy way to do this. Compare the results to what you thought it would do (which you should have written down or diagrammed on a piece of paper first). Work with small (exam-sized) test cases to keep this tractable. Visualization is always an effective study aid for any subject.
