1) A bidder's value will never raise, but their bid may - the two are separate

2) If it is possible for a bidder to improve their payoff by raising their bet, then they will do so - in general, bidders will always seek a better payoff, with their bid as a ways to achieve that.

3) What do you mean by "risky"? There isn't really a sense of "risk" in this problem - we are simply analyzing whether we're at an equilibrium (where we assume we know all aspects, including private bids, in order to analyze, so there is no unknown information or risk)

Author: Aidan McNay (student)

Vote Count: 0

The bids are given in "per click" form (we never have a "Clicks-Per-Value" notion). They are independent of values.

Yes, that is the definition of being at equilibrium - you should prove that no matter how much one raises/lowers their bid, they never get a better payoff. You have to show this in general (for any increase/decrease), but it is helpful to note that a buyer's payoff only changes when their bid changes which item they get.

On a side note, we don't have any notion of 1st and 2nd values - each buyer only has one value. A buyer's payoff can be expressed as

CTR_{\text{item received}}\cdot\left(\text{Value}_{per\ click}-\text{Price}_{per\ click}\right)
