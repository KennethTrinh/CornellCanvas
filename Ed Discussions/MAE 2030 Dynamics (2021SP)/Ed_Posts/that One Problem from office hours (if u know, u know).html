Marigot Fackenthal I think this works, but probably not everyone's usual flavor: (Also note that the eigenvalue has mu^2+1, not mu^2-1; as a check nothing funky in terms of bifurcations should happen at anything other than mu=0) R''\left(t\right)=R\left(t\right)\omega^2-2\mu\omega R'\left(t\right) R''\left(\omega t\right)=R\left(\omega t\right)-2\mu R'\left(\omega t\right) R(0)=R_0,\qquad R'(0)=0 v\left(t\right)=R'\left(t\right),\qquad R\left(t\right)=R_0+\int_{_0}^tv\left(\tau\right)d\tau v'\left(\omega t\right)=R_0 + \int_0^tv\left(\omega \tau\right)d\tau-2\mu v\left(\omega t\right) \text{Laplace transform:}\quad sV(s)=\frac{R_0}{s} + \frac{V(s)}{s}-2\mu V(s) V(s)=\frac{R_0}{s^2+2\mu s-1} \text{Inverse Laplace transform:}\quad \frac{v(\omega t)}{R_0} = \frac{e^{-\mu \omega t}}{\lambda}\text{}\sinh(\lambda\omega t),\quad \lambda=\sqrt{\mu^2+1} Addendum: if mu is small, we have approximately \frac{v(\omega t)}{R_0}\sim\left(1-\mu\omega t\right)\sinh\left(\omega t\right)+O\left(\mu^2\right) and we also see the solution for when mu=0, R''=R, \text{ or } v''=v \text{ with } v(0)=0, \text{ which has }v(\omega t)=\sinh(\omega t) We can consider the converse when mu is large, but that's a bit more work to obtain the approximation. We can, however, verify that when mu becomes infinite that the mass does not move at all.
