A periodic motion means that your state (position and velocity) at time 0 (or t for any t) equals to the state at time period T (or t+T).

NOT the time derivative of state (velocity and acceleration)

If you have velocity at 0 = velocity at T AND acceleration at 0 = accel at T

no guarantee that the position at 0 equals to position at T.

------------------------

A counter example to illustrate: let's say a central force of ~(-r^3)

at time 0, r,theta=10, 0. with some velocity e.g. (1,2)

at time "period" T, your r, theta=10, 1, with the same velocity e.g. (1,2)

at these two time because the r is the same, the force (at 0 &T) are equal, and the acceleraion are equal. HOWEVER, this T is not your period, because your position (r, theta) at 0 and T are different. It also does NOT necessarily show that your motion is periodical.

So, saying that the state derivative at 0 equals to state derivative at T does NOT guarantee the motion is periodic. (by the argument or also the counter example).

On the contrary, having your state at 0 equals to state at T GUARANTEES that the state derivative at 0 equals to the state derivative at T. BECAUSE your state derivative just depends on the state, so if the states are equal then the state derivative will equal as well.

So the direction is one way, not two ways (if and only if)

---

If you want to use the derivative of state (Xdot), then you need to make sure that

Xdot at t = Xdot at t+T FOR ALL t from 0 to T

NOT JUST

Xdot at 0 = Xdot at T
