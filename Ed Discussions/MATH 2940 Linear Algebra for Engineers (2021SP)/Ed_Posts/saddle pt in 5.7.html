I'm having trouble understanding how negative eigenvalues represent attraction. Given a negative eigenvalue of magnitude >1, aren't we repelled from the origin, because as k grows large, the negative eigenvalue's magnitude grows large?

For instance, given $\lambda_1 = -2$ wouldn't the solution jump back and forth through the origin as we increment k, since $(-2)^1 = -2$, and $(-2)^2 = 4$, so to go from k=1 to k=2 we approach the origin and exit twice as far out?

You're confusing the conditions of differential equation (5.7) and dynamical system (5.6). They are not the same.

In differential equations, the eigenvalues appear in the exponent of the general solution

$$\vec{x}(t) = c_1e^{\lambda_1t}\vec{v}_1 + c_2e^{\lambda_2t}\vec{v}_2$$

Hence attraction occurs on $Span\{\vec{v}_i\}$ as $t \rightarrow \infty$ if $\lambda_i<0$.

Contrast this with the dynamical system where the eigenvalue is the base of the exponent itself

$$\vec{x}_k = c_1\lambda_1^k\vec{v}_1 + c_2\lambda_2^k\vec{v}_2$$

where attraction occurs on $Span\{\vec{v}_i\}$ as $k \rightarrow \infty$ if $|\lambda_i|<1$.
