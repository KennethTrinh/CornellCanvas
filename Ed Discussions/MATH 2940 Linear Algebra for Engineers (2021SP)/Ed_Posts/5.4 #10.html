$T(\vec{b}_i)$ means you take basis polynomial $i$ and plug in the four values for $t$ to get the four entries of the output vector. Hence for example, $$T(t^2) = \begin{bmatrix}(-3)^2 \\ (-1)^2 \\ 1^2 \\ 3^2\end{bmatrix}$$ Whenever you write a column vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ as $$\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix}$$ you are implicitly writing its coordinates under the standard basis $\mathcal{E}$ since $$\begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix} = x_1\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0\end{bmatrix} + x_2\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0\end{bmatrix} + \ldots + x_n\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 1\end{bmatrix} \implies [\begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix}]_\mathcal{E} = \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n\end{bmatrix}$$ Hence $$[T(t^2)]_\mathcal{E}=[\begin{bmatrix}(-3)^2 \\ (-1)^2 \\ 1^2 \\ 3^2\end{bmatrix}]_\mathcal{E} = \begin{bmatrix}(-3)^2 \\ (-1)^2 \\ 1^2 \\ 3^2\end{bmatrix}$$
