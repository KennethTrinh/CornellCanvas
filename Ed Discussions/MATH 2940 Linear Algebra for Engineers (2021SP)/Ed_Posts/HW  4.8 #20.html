Author: Phillip Jang (staff)

Vote Count: 1

A difference equation imposes a regular pattern that a sequence must satisfy by relating any sequence value to its immediate successor values. When all the $y_{k+i}$ terms appear in a linear equation, the solutions will (usually) take the form $y_k = r^k$ where $r$ is possibly a complex number. The sine and cosine appear when you have complex roots to the auxiliary equation (see p.250 and also check out Euler's formula). These appear because you need some sort of sign reversal every two (or more) time steps for the terms in the difference equation to cancel out, and sine and cosine periodically flip signs depending on their frequency. A negative real number $r$ flips signs every single step of $k$ while sine and cosine can take longer to flip signs. The discrete sequence comes from setting t to the integers t=1,2,3,... and the exact repetition of the discretely sampled sequence of cos values is a byproduct of the sine and cosine with frequency $\pi/4$ having the right frequency to repeat on integer values of $t$ and flip signs every two time steps.
