This comes from Euler's formula: $e^{i\theta}=\cos \theta+i\sin \theta$ applied to $\theta=\pi/2$.

Author: Alekos Robotis (staff)

Vote Count: 0

You can think of $\cos \theta+i\sin \theta$ as parametrizing the unit circle in $\Bbb{C}$ (which is isomorphic to $\Bbb{R}^2$). The parametrization goes counterclockwise from the positive real axis ($x-$axis) and since $i$ is located on the positive imaginary axis ($y-$axis), we see that the angle should be $\theta=\frac{\pi}{2}$ to have $e^{i\theta}=i$. Alternatively, you can just see that $\cos\frac{\pi}{2}=0$ and $\sin \frac{\pi}{2}=1$, and then plug in to the formula I wrote above.
