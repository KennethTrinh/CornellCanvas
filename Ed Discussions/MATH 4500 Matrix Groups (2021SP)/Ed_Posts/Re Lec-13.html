Different people use different conventions. If you choose $w_j=a_j-ib_j$ then the matrix $R_j$ is $$R_j=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta_j&-\sin\theta_j\\\sin\theta_j&\cos\theta_j\end{pmatrix}.$$ If you choose $w_j=a_j+ib_j$ then the matrix $R_j$ is $$R_j=\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta_j&\sin\theta_j\\-\sin\theta_j&\cos\theta_j\end{pmatrix}.$$ Once you make a choice, it is best to stick to it. Both conventions say that the matrix $R_j$ represents a rotation through $\theta_j$ in the plane spanned by $a_j$ and $b_j$. But I prefer the first convention, because it says that the rotation is positive (counterclockwise) with respect to the oriented basis $a_j$, $b_j$. Incidentally, one thing I forgot to mention in lecture is that the vectors $a_j$ and $b_j$ are orthogonal but not normalized: we have $\|a_j\|=\|b_j\|=1/\sqrt2$, so we have to multiply both by $\sqrt2$ to get an orthonormal basis.
