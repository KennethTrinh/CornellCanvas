Author: Reyer Sjamaar (admin)

Vote Count: 1

The eigenvector decomposition of $v$ is not exactly the same as the coordinates of $v$ in an eigenbasis. Here is a simple example, where $A$ is a diagonal matrix. Let $$A=\begin{pmatrix}2&&&&\\&2&&&\\&&-7&&\\&&&-7&\\&&&&-7\end{pmatrix},\qquad v=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\3\\4\\5\end{pmatrix}.$$ Then $A$ has two distinct eigenvalues, namely $\lambda_1=2$ (of multiplicity $2$) and $\lambda_2=-7$ (of multiplicity $3$). So $k=2$, while $n=5$. The eigenspace for the eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ is $$V_1=\mathrm{span}(e_1,e_2).$$ The eigenspace for the eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ is $$V_2=\mathrm{span}(e_3,e_4,e_5).$$ The vector $v$ has eigenvector decomposition $v=v_1+v_2$, where $$v_1=\begin{pmatrix}1\\2\\0\\0\\0\end{pmatrix}\in V_1,\qquad v_2=\begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\3\\4\\5\end{pmatrix}\in V_2.$$

Yes. Â The technical term is that $\mathbf{R}^5$ is the direct sum of the eigenspaces of $A$. Whenever you have a vector space $V$ and a diagonal linear transformation $A$ from $V$ to itself, $V$ is the direct sum of all the eigenspaces $E_\lambda$ of $A$. Here $E_\lambda$ is defined as the set of all $v\in V$ with $Av=\lambda v$.
