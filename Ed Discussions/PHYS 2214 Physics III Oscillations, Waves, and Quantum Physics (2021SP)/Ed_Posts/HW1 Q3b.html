I think I might have an idea about the math issue. The full expression for the displacement is

x\left(t\right)=Ae^{-\frac{t}{\tau_A}}\cos\left(\sqrt{\omega_0^2-\frac{1}{\tau_A^2}}t+\phi\right)

If you want to directly calculate the velocity, taking a time derivative of this expression would give you two terms: one from taking the derivative of the exponential and the other from taking the derivative of the cosine. I expect that you might have considered only the first term, while for an underdamped oscillator the larger term actually comes from the derivative of the cosine.

Working from this formula doesn't really help to address the physics question as brought into focus by Cameron, though. The question asks you to consider physically why the oscillation frequency is reduced from

\omega_0\ to\ \sqrt{\omega_0^2-\frac{1}{\tau_A^2}}.

If an oscillator is traveling away from its equilibrium position, heading toward a maximum, and turning around back toward its equilibrium position,, will more damping increase or decrease the time it takes to pass back past the equilibrium position?
