"... if the grapefruit is displaced a small amount and released" is the key phrase here. Essentially, if you imagine that this grapefruit is in between two hills and you displace it from equilibrium a little bit such that it is up one of the hills. Assuming no friction, this grapefruit will "oscillate" by rolling down the hill, past the equilibrium point, and up the other hill. When it is up one of the hills (i.e. the initial displacement), there is a net force and a displacement from equilibrium.

Author: Dan Ralph (admin)

The formula you listed says that near a stable equilibrium point the force is equal to a negative constant times the displacement from the equilibrium position. I think the key point that you may be missing is that the negative constant corresponds to the slope \frac{dF}{dx} evaluated at the equilibrium point, not the value of the force at the equilibrium point.
