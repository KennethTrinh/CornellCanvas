Yes, the approach you describe is promising. The wave function in your question is missing the imaginary 'i' in the exponent, but perhaps that omission is only in your posted question and not on your actual work. The derivatives, once you have them, can be used in the Schrodinger equation. You should be left with a collection of constants that as a group take the place of m-tilda. Evaluate that group of constants and find they result in a dimensionless, pure number value of 0.131.

Here's another approach: Rather than starting with a wave function guess, work directly with the Schrodinger equation itself. Instead of taking the first time derivative in the equation, take the derivative with respect to the dimensionless time--that is 'dt' becomes 'd(h*t-tilda/E). The h/E constants can be brought out from the derivative. Similarly for the second x-derivative: instead take the derivative with respect to a*x-tilda.
