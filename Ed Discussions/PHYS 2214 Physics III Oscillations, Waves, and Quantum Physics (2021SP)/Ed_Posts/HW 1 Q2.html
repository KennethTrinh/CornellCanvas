There are different logical paths by which you can determine the sign of the phase in this problem. Regarding the hint given in the statement of the problem, if the wave displacement has the form $x(t) = A cos(\omega t +\phi)$, then the velocity is $dx/dt = -A \omega sin(\omega t + \phi)$, and the velocity evaluated at time $t=0$ is $dx/dt = -A \omega sin(\phi)$. To match the positive velocity at $t=0$ in the graph, you therefore need $sin(\phi)$ to have a negative value (assuming that $A$ is positive), or that $\phi$ is between 0 and $-\pi$.

The logic of the answer given in the solution set is that the peak positive amplitude of the wave occurs from the graph at $t \approx +0.05$ ms. The peak amplitude of the wave occurs when the argument of the cosine function, $\omega t +\phi$, is equal to zero. Therefore if the time $t$ is positive for this condition, the phase $\phi$ must be negative. Both logical approaches give a consistent answer.
