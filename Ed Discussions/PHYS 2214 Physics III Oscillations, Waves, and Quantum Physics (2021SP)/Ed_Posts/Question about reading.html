Author: Dan Ralph (admin)

Oliver's answer is correct. I also think it can be helpful to draw a picture. Draw out the original wave as a function of position at some time t=0, and then draw its position when it is shifted slightly at a slightly later time. Motion of an individual element of the string must always be straight up and down since we are dealing with a transverse wave, so you can get an idea of how the velocity $dy/dt$ varies with position by drawing vertical arrows connecting the original and shifted waves. From this sort of drawing you can see that when the slope $dy/dx$ of the waveform is close to zero then the velocity $dy/dt$ is also close to zero, because the original and shifted waveforms will nearly overlap at such positions.
